LEGPHEL PMS IMPLEMENTATION REFERENCE 18 APR 2026

A visual map
of the nine-stage
engagement lifecycle.

This document maps the architecture of LEGPHEL PMS — its nine operational stages, its actors, its entities, and the mechanics that carry an engagement from first inquiry to sealed closure. It is a visual reference for the implementation team.

S1 · S2 · S3
Pre-commitment
Inquiry capture, commercial shaping, reservation setup. No inventory lock yet — only holds with expiry.
S4 · S5 · S6
Commit & arrive
Formal confirmation, pre-arrival readiness, check-in. The commitment boundary is S4.
S7 · S8 · S9
Live & closure
Active stay with live folio, settled checkout, post-stay closure. Records seal on S9.
01
Layer One · Foundations

System architecture
& foundational scaffolding.

Before the stages. The build chain that governs every specification decision, the five engagement types that pass through the single lifecycle, the three-level entity hierarchy that anchors every operational record, the authority tiers that govern action, and the abstracted technology stack beneath it all.

1.1
Five use types, one lifecycle

LEGPHEL governs five engagement use types. All five pass through the same S1–S9 state machine. Behavioural variation by use type occurs within each stage — it does not create parallel lifecycles or alternative stage sequences. Combined engagements (e.g. accommodation plus conference under one guest identity) are a single Inquiry holding multiple Entries, each progressing independently.

1.2
Core entity hierarchy — Inquiry · Entry · Segment

Every operational record anchors to one of three entities in a fixed hierarchy. Inquiry is the commercial container. Entry is the lifecycle unit — it progresses independently through S1–S9. Segment records re-entry: when an entry returns to an earlier stage, a new Segment is created and prior segments seal read-only. Mutation is additive only — no operational record is ever deleted.

INQUIRY
Created at S1 when a guest or agent makes initial contact. One Inquiry may hold multiple Entries under a single guest identity.
ENTRY
The lifecycle unit. Progresses independently through S1–S9. Every reservation, folio, handoff, dispute, and work order anchors to an Entry.
SEGMENT
One Segment per pass through the stages. Re-entry creates a new Segment; prior Segments seal as read-only history.
1.3
The actor model — operational tiers & configuration authority

Every operational action in the system carries actor attribution — no action is unattributed. Three operational tiers (L1 custodian → L2 FOM → L3 GM) govern day-to-day workflow. L0 system actors and the AI agent act without human input at Tier 1 equivalent. L4 Admin is a separate authority surface governing configuration only — not operational records. OTA is external with no direct write access.

1.4
Technology stack & abstraction boundaries

All external integrations are accessed through named interfaces. No service calls an external API directly. The underlying technology for each integration can be swapped without changing service logic — this is an architectural constraint, not a preference.

02
Layer Two · The Lifecycle

The nine-stage engagement lifecycle —
from inquiry to sealed closure.

Every engagement passes through the same nine operational stages. The stages are the structural backbone of the system. Forward transitions advance the engagement; re-entry transitions create new segments when a stage must be re-executed. S4 is the commitment boundary; S6 is where the folio goes live; S9 is terminal.

2.0
Master flow · S1 → S9 with re-entry topology

Forward path (solid) shows the intended progression. Re-entry paths (dashed) show where an entry may return to an earlier stage, producing a new segment and sealing the prior one. The walk-in compressed path bypasses normal pre-arrival preparation with all S5 obligations auto-fulfilled at check-in. The S5 no-show path terminates at S9-equivalent financial closure.

COMMITMENT BOUNDARY
S4 freezes commercial terms as a binding commitment snapshot. Everything before S4 is preparation; everything after is execution of the promise made here. Post-S4 changes require re-entry and new segments.
FOLIO LIFECYCLE
Folio is created at S3 (PROVISIONAL). Remains PROVISIONAL through S4–S5. Converts to LIVE at S6 — irreversible. Settles at S8. Seals at S9 closure.
S9 IS TERMINAL
No stage follows S9. Post-closure intervention (L2 additive records, L3 corrections) does not re-open S9 — it adds records on top of the sealed archive.

03a
Layer Three (a) · Pre-Commitment Deep-Dives

Inside S1 · S2 · S3 —
inquiry, negotiation, reservation setup.

Three detailed per-stage flowcharts. Each traces the operational sequence from entry routes through the main orchestration (with actor-tier color coding), surfaces decision points and exception paths, and terminates at the stage's exit routes. All three stages are pre-commitment — no inventory is locked, no folio is live, no commercial term is frozen. S4 is the boundary.

3a.1
S1 · Inquiry & Configuration — operational flow

S1 captures what the guest wants and produces at least one validated availability configuration. It is primarily an L1 custodian stage; L2 and L3 appear only on specific escalations (ambiguous duplicate, post-S4 re-entry, DEFICIENT decisions above custodian scope). No commitment, no hold, no payment, no folio — these are architectural forbiddens at S1.

S1 IS L1-DOMINATED
Almost all S1 operations are L1 custodian actions. L2 FOM appears only for ambiguous duplicate resolution, DEFICIENT decisions above custodian scope, and re-entry from S3+. L3 GM only for re-entry from S4+ under specific triggers.
OTA_SOURCE IMMUTABILITY
If the source channel is OTA, the otaSource flag is set at Entry creation and is immutable through the entire lifecycle. It cannot be added retrospectively after S1 exit.
DUPLICATE DETECTION IS A GATE
An unresolved duplicate flag blocks S1 exit unconditionally. One of three resolution paths must be recorded: merge, acknowledge-and-proceed, or dismiss-with-reason.
3a.2
S2 · Negotiation & Quotation — operational flow

S2 shapes the commercial terms and produces the accepted quotation that S3 will build on. Discount approval and speculative hold volume thresholds drive the most escalation traffic. Authority tiers cascade: L1 within front-desk threshold → L2 FOM above → L3 GM for rates below MSR or FOC on group quotations. Every rate flows through the PricingPipelineEngine — manual rate entry that bypasses it is forbidden.

S2 AUTO-FULFILMENT IS NOT A SKIP
When a guest accepts a standard package rate without negotiation, the state machine records S2 as auto-fulfilled with a TraceEvent. The entry proceeds S1→S3. The audit trail shows S2 passage — the stage is not bypassed, just compressed to evidence.
VALIDITY vs ACKNOWLEDGEMENT WINDOW
Two separate timers. Validity (W15) governs when the quoted rate lapses. Ack window tracks guest silence. Validity expiring forces revalidation; ack window timing out creates an open loop that requires documented resolution at S2 exit.
DISCOUNT BLOCKING IS ABSOLUTE
An unapproved discount blocks S2 exit unconditionally — not overridable even with GM presence. Each discount must carry its matching authority TraceEvent.
3a.3
S3 · Reservation Setup — operational flow

S3 is where the reservation's operational and financial foundations are laid without crossing the commitment boundary. Committed hold, provisional folio, billing model fixation, advance payment or credit extension with ceiling, cancellation disclosure — all must be in place before S4. The re-entry back-flows (S3→S2 and S3→S1) behave asymmetrically: S3→S2 retains the hold and preserves PIs; S3→S1 releases the hold and supersedes PIs.

HOLD = COMMITMENT WITH EXPIRY
A committed hold is a strong claim with a running timer — it is not a lock. Inventory remains releasable if S4 confirmation doesn't occur before expiry. Locking is exclusively S4's act.
FOLIO IS SINGLE-PER-ENTRY
The Entry-level provisional folio is created once and persists through every segment. FolioService.getOrCreate is the sole entry point; duplicate folio creation on re-entry is forbidden.
CREDIT EXTENSION REQUIRES CEILING
A CreditExtensionCeilingRecord without a non-null ceilingAmount is a structurally incomplete approval. The service blocks the record creation. Unbounded credit is architecturally forbidden.
03b
Layer Three (b) · Commit & Arrival Deep-Dives

Inside S4 · S5 · S6 —
confirmation, pre-arrival, check-in.

Three detailed per-stage flowcharts covering the commitment boundary (S4), pre-arrival readiness with no-show determination (S5), and the irreversible folio-to-LIVE moment at check-in (S6). Between S4 and the end of S6, three architecturally critical state changes occur: commercial terms freeze as a commitment snapshot, inventory transitions CONFIRMED → OCCUPIED, and the provisional folio converts to live.

3b.1
S4 · Confirmation & Ownership — operational flow

S4 is the commitment boundary. One atomic transaction confirms the reservation, freezes commercial terms as a denormalised snapshot, transitions inventory to CONFIRMED, creates H1, generates and dispatches the confirmation voucher, and registers the pre-arrival countdown timer. Overbooking detection runs mid-flow and halts the transaction until GM approval is recorded. GM authority is mandatory for all overbooking regardless of trigger type.

ATOMICITY IS THE WHOLE POINT
Reservation creation, hold confirmation, inventory lock, H1 creation, voucher generation and dispatch, and timer registration — all in one transaction. A partial commit (reservation created but voucher never dispatched) is a structural defect, not a bug.
OVERBOOKING GATE
GM approval is mandatory for every overbooking regardless of trigger type. Trigger type is inferred from Entry.otaSource at detection time and immutable once set. Manual reclassification OTA_CONFLICT → DELIBERATE is architecturally forbidden.
S4 DOES NOT CALL PRICING
PricingPipelineEngine.resolve is not invoked at S4 — the frozen rate carried from S2 is the authority. Pricing is only invoked when re-entry has routed back through S2, in which case the new quotation's rate is what gets frozen on the new Reservation.
3b.2
S5 · Pre-Arrival / Pre-Event — operational flow

S5 is a readiness stage that runs on timers. The pre-arrival task checklist is seeded at S5 activation (W4) — nine task types, each PENDING until COMPLETE or WAIVED with recorded reason. Room assignment occurs here. The no-show cutoff (W5) runs in parallel and produces a governed sub-process within the stage — never an auto-terminal outcome. FOM determination is the only path to no-show terminal closure.

NO-SHOW IS NEVER AUTOMATIC
The cutoff timer only triggers the inquiry. The determination is a recorded FOM decision event — W5 firing without FOM follow-through keeps the entry in (ACTIVE, S5) indefinitely. No code path transitions an entry to no-show terminal state based on time alone.
H1 ACCEPTED ≠ H1 FULFILLED
Acceptance means the front desk has taken responsibility. Fulfilment means all readiness evidence is recorded. The S5→S6 guard requires FULFILLED — accepting H1 without recording evidence blocks progression.
W34 HANDOFF ON ACTIVATION
Advance payment follow-up (W34) is cancelled in the same transaction as the S4→S5 stage write. Follow-up responsibility transfers to S5 reconciliation. If W34 fires after S5 activation it is a timing bug.
3b.3
S6 · Check-In & Stay Initiation — operational flow

S6 is a single atomic check-in transaction. Identity verification gates everything — four paths (first-time · returning valid · returning expired · VIP) each produce a mandatory verification event. The folio converts PROVISIONAL → LIVE (irreversible). Inventory claim CONFIRMED → OCCUPIED. H1 closes; H2 (housekeeping) and H3 (F&B) are created with W25 acceptance timers. Only one forward path exists (S6 → S7); room change at check-in is a re-entry to S1 with a compressed new segment.

LIVE IS ONE-WAY
Once Folio.state = LIVE, the folio never reverts to PROVISIONAL — through any re-entry, amendment, rate change, billing model change, or any other mechanism. This is the single hardest architectural rule at S6. Everything downstream depends on it.
VIP IS ALTERNATIVE, NOT SKIP
The VIP path substitutes the verification mechanism (booking-relationship confirmation instead of counter-level document check) but not the verification event. Every identity path — including VIP — writes a verification event on the GuestProfile.
WALK-IN: NO H1 AT ALL
On the walk-in path, there is no phantom H1 created and immediately closed. The compressed S5 auto-fulfilment explicitly records a no-H1 path. H1 exists only where a reservations-to-front-desk handoff actually occurred.
03c
Layer Three (c) · Live & Closure Deep-Dives

Inside S7 · S8 · S9 —
the live stay, checkout, final closure.

Three detailed per-stage flowcharts covering the live-stay governance regime (S7), the departure and settlement transaction (S8), and the terminal closure with loop closure invariant (S9). These stages operate on the live folio — the architectural rule that governs S6 onward is that LIVE never reverts to PROVISIONAL. S9 is terminal; post-closure access exists but no stage reopens.

3c.1
S7 · Stay / Event Management — operational flow

S7 is continuous, not a one-shot transition. It runs for the entire duration of the stay with daily night audit cycles, charge postings via the pricing & tax pipeline, credit ceiling monitoring, DEFICIENT condition management, and dispute handling. Every amendment — rate, inclusion, billing model, date extension, room change — routes through a re-entry back to S1/S2/S3/S4 with a new segment. In-place edits are forbidden.

AMENDMENT BY RE-ENTRY · NEVER IN-PLACE
Every in-stay change — rate revision, date extension, room change, billing model change — routes through a re-entry transition with a new segment (except S7→S3 simple folio adjustments and S8→S7 charge posting). Direct field edits to rate, reservation terms, or folio lines are architecturally forbidden.
NIGHT AUDIT IS A SEAL
A completed NightAuditRecord for an operating date is immutable. Post-audit manual posting to that date is forbidden. Catch-up postings live on the actual posting date with referenceDate pointing back — the original audit stands.
CREDIT CEILING · 100% IS A SOFT GATE
At 100% ceiling, non-mandatory charge postings block pending FOM acknowledgement. Mandatory ROOM_CHARGE lines (night audit) always post through — the hotel does not stop charging rent because the guest's credit is maxed. The soft gate protects non-essential postings only.
3c.2
S8 · Checkout & Settlement — operational flow

S8 executes the departure transaction. The folio is compiled, verified against H4 returns, and presented for settlement. Three settlement outcomes — SETTLED, OUTSTANDING, or the re-entry exceptions. Room transitions through DEPARTED_DIRTY (never directly to DEPARTED_CLEAN). Inspection may be deferred to W9. The critical difference from S7→S8: the S8→S9 dispute gate returns BLOCKED with no override path.

NO OVERRIDE AT S8→S9
At S7→S8 the dispute gate returns BLOCKED_WITH_OVERRIDE_AVAILABLE and GM may invoke an override via DisputeGateOverrideRecord. At S8→S9 the engine returns a plain BLOCKED — hardcoded, no configuration switch, no authority level. The dispute must reach RESOLVED or CLOSED before the entry can close. GM's role at S8→S9 is to close the dispute, not to override the gate.
INVENTORY CLAIM LAGS THE PHYSICAL STATE
At S8 the room's physical state transitions to DEPARTED_DIRTY, but the inventory claim remains CONFIRMED. The claim does not release until S9 closure. A DEPARTED_DIRTY room with an S8-settled entry cannot be reassigned to another booking until S9 fully closes.
OUTSTANDING IS A TERMINAL FOR S8 · NOT A FAILURE
OUTSTANDING is a governed outcome, not a partial success. It means the folio closed S8 with an unreconciled balance that S9 will pursue via W8. The balance is documented, visible, and trackable — this is the audit-integrity path. Auto-SETTLED with a remaining balance is the forbidden pattern.
3c.3
S9 · Post-Stay & Closure — operational flow

S9 is terminal — no stage follows. Three populations enter (standard departure from S8, no-show from S5, cancelled pre-arrival). The Loop Closure Invariant enumerates every open loop that must have a resolution path before EntryService.close may proceed. On closure the entire engagement record seals — all segments, folio, folio lines, night audits, disputes, handoffs become immutable. Post-closure access exists at L2/L3 but never reopens the record.

LOOP CLOSURE IS ENFORCED BY THE CODE, NOT BY POLICY
EntryService.close does not proceed if any of the eleven invariant conditions is unsatisfied. This is checked in the method body, not left to process discipline. An agent-mediated entry with a configured commission rate cannot close without a CommissionDueRecord — the service raises StageGateBlockedError before writing anything.
CLOSED IS IRREVERSIBLE
There is no reopen path. Post-closure access (L1 read, L2 additive, L3 corrections) operates on top of the sealed archive — no pre-closure record is ever amended. If a correction is required, it lives as a new record with its own date and rationale. This is the hotel's permanent institutional record of the engagement.
OUTSTANDING IS A VALID TERMINAL
An entry may reach EntryStatus.CLOSED with Folio.state = OUTSTANDING. The W8 PaymentFollowUp worker continues after closure, writing additively. This is the governed path for unpaid balances — the alternative is write-off, which requires GM authority and a recorded reason. Silent balance disappearance is forbidden.
04
Layer Four · Actor-Authority View

Who may act — at what tier,
under what gate, across which stage.

Every governed action in LEGPHEL has a minimum authority. The Actor-Authority Matrix (LOCKED) fixes those minima. This layer reads the Matrix back in visual form, in two parts. Part A — authority orientation (delivered this session): the tier ladder and its scope, the cross-cutting absolute forbiddens, and the stage × tier density heatmap — who exists, what is forbidden everywhere, and where authority actually concentrates. Part B — authority dynamics (next session): the three recurring escalation shapes, the cross-stage mechanism authority chains, and the AI agent model.

4.1
Tier ladder & scope — L0 · L1 · L2 · L3 · L4 · EXT

Five in-system tiers plus one external class. L0–L3 form an ascending operational ladder inside the system. L4 is off-axis — distinct configuration surface; not a higher rung on the same ladder. EXT is external — initiates requests but never touches records directly; every EXT action is mediated by an L1+ actor.

ASCENDING vs OFF-AXIS
L0 through L3 form a single ascending ladder — higher tiers may perform everything lower tiers can, plus their own additional scope. L4 is NOT above L3. It operates on a different axis: configuration. An Admin Console session cannot post a charge or close a dispute no matter who holds the credentials.
EXT HAS NO SYSTEM AUTHORITY
Coordinators, agents, and guests initiate requests but produce no records themselves. Every external request becomes a system fact only when an L1+ actor captures it. This is why forbids direct coordinator access and records amendments "on coordinator's instruction" rather than "by coordinator."
GM AUTHORITY IS WITHIN THE SYSTEM
The rule is absolute: the GM cannot approve informally, verbally, or outside a governed record. L3 authority only exists inside the system. An action approved in corridor, by SMS, or by nod-through is not an L3 approval — it is no approval.
L0 IS A FIRST-CLASS ACTOR
System actions — night audit posting, timer expiry, auto-seal, ambient threshold notices — are not "automatic plumbing." They are actor records attributable to the system, auditable, and bounded. When L0 acts, the event is governed just like a human action, which is why silent mutations (anywhere, anyone) are a cross-cutting forbidden.
4.2
Cross-cutting absolute forbiddens — no actor, no tier, no override

The eleven Part-1 rules of the Actor-Authority Matrix. These apply across all stages and all mechanisms — they do not escalate, do not admit exceptions, and do not attach to any authority tier. Grouped here by kind: structural integrity (4), communication & data governance (4), AI agent constraints (3).

WHY GROUPED THIS WAY
Cluster A protects the shape of the process — gates, commitments, approvals. Cluster B protects the integrity of the record — what got said, what got kept, what got dated. Cluster C carves out the AI agent's specific surface, which is narrow on purpose. Cluster membership is a reading aid; enforcement is uniform across all eleven.
INHERITANCE INTO STAGE FORBIDDENS
Each of S1–S9 publishes its own FORBIDDEN list (e.g. S1 "communicate final rate", S4 "modify commercial terms in-place post-freeze", S8 "modify sealed folio lines"). Those are stage-specific expressions of the cross-cutting rules — not additions. The cross-cutting set is the floor.
WHY AI IS A DEDICATED CLUSTER
The AI agent is an L0 actor with unusually wide surface area — it can see everything, draft everything, propose everything. The three forbiddens carve out the missing ingredient: autonomous action. This is the design choice that makes human-in-the-loop a property of the system, not a property of trust settings.
RULE COUNT & SOURCE
The matrix lists eleven rules in Part 1. The four plus four plus three grouping reflects the locked set exactly. No twelfth rule is hiding; the grid shape here is semantic, not numerological.
4.3
Stage × authority heatmap — where each tier is most present

Cell fill is raw row count from the matrix — the number of distinct actions at a stage whose minimum authority is that tier. Rows with dual-tier floors (e.g. "L2 or L3") are counted at the lower floor. All-actors FORBIDDEN rows are excluded — they belong to 4.2. Two patterns stand out: S4 is L0-dominant (a commitment boundary run mostly by the system), and S7 is the only stage with heavy presence across four tiers (the live-stay surface).

READING THE S4 SPIKE
S4 is where the entry becomes commitment. Of the eleven authority-bearing actions at S4, four are system-performed: freezing the commercial snapshot, locking inventory to CONFIRMED, running overbooking detection, and typing OTA_CONFLICT via OTA_SOURCE. Humans set up the decision — the boundary itself fires automatically. This is the architectural reason S4 cannot be "manually overridden."
READING THE S7 DIVERSITY
S7 spans the live stay. Night audit (L0) runs alongside at-service charge posting (L1), 100%-ceiling FOM acknowledgements (L2), rate-change Path-3 amendments, dispute closes, and Tier-3 recovery (L3). The S7 column is the widest surface in the product — which is why SIG-S7 is the longest SIG and S7 has the most cross-stage touch points.
WHY L4 IS UNIFORMLY ZERO
The L4 row reads zero across all nine operational stages. That is not a gap — it is the off-axis property of 4.1 rendered numerically. L4 actions configure the rules under which S1–S9 run; they do not appear as rows inside S1–S9 because Admin Console does not execute operational workflow. The Admin Console authority rows are specified separately in the matrix.
COUNTING RULE
Raw row count, with dual-floor rows (e.g. "L2 or L3") counted at the lower tier and "L0 system auto / L1 fallback" rows counted as L0. All-actors FORBIDDEN rows are excluded — they appear in 4.2. Per-tier totals on the right confirm: L1 is the broadest operational tier (62 rows), L2 second (37), L3 reserved for exceptions (19), L0 automating the seams (22), L4 uniformly absent.
4.4
Escalation patterns — the three shapes that recur across the Matrix

Escalation is not ad-hoc per stage. Three structural shapes recur, each with its own trigger logic and authority topology. Pattern 1 — threshold-bracketed: authority escalates with magnitude (discount, rate override, credit ceiling). Pattern 2 — stage-gated: the same action changes authority as the engagement progresses (cancellation across the commitment boundary). Pattern 3 — L3 exclusive: actions with one admissible actor only (GM) because the blast radius is irreversibly large.

WHY THESE SHAPES EXIST
Authority is a function of blast radius, not hierarchy rank. Pattern 1 tracks financial magnitude; Pattern 2 tracks consequence weight across the commitment boundary at S4; Pattern 3 tracks irreversibility. Tier = "how far can this action reach if wrong", not "how senior is the actor".
PATTERN 2 AND RE-ENTRY
When a stage is re-entered — e.g. S7→S2 for rate renegotiation via amendment Path 3 — the re-entered stage's authority still applies to the re-entered logic, but the enclosing operational context remains S7. A re-entry back through S2 does not become an S2 action; it remains an S7 event executed through S2's pricing machinery. Authority rules compose rather than replace.
PATTERN 3 IS A DESIGN CHOICE
The system could expose a gradient — e.g. write-offs under Y require only L2. It deliberately does not. Write-off, dispute closure, and commercial adjustment entry are irreversible record-creating acts; distributing them would trade small operational friction for large governance risk. The commercial adjustment entry row is the single row in the matrix marked explicitly "not delegatable" — the strongest marker of irreversibility.
4.5
Cross-stage mechanism authority — five chains across the lifecycle

Each row is a cross-stage mechanism. Each cell is the minimum authority required at that stage × mechanism intersection. Dashed cells mean the mechanism does not fire at that stage. These five are the mechanisms whose authority topology changes across the commitment boundary at S4 — which is why they exhibit the most structural complexity. The remaining Part 3 mechanisms (early check-in / late checkout, group, room moves, night audit, walk-in) are either stage-local or single-tier; their flow is captured in Layer 3, not here.

WHY THESE FIVE
Part 3 has thirteen mechanisms in total. These five are specifically the ones that cross the commitment boundary at S4 and therefore exhibit the most authority-topology complexity. The remaining eight are either stage-local (early check-in, walk-in), single-tier (group, room moves within stay), or infrastructural (night audit) — their flows are captured in Layer 3's stage-level flowcharts, not here.
THE COMMITMENT BOUNDARY IS VISIBLE IN EVERY ROW
Run your eye down the S3→S4 divider. In four of five mechanisms the tier floor steps up: L1/L2 → L2 (cancellation), L1 → L1 but with heavier oversight (service recovery), and overbooking/no-show become present for the first time. Amendment is the sole mechanism whose S4 cell reads FORBIDDEN — because at S4 the snapshot is being created; it cannot be concurrently amended. Once past S4, amendment resumes as the primary surface for any post-commitment change.
NO-SHOW AND OVERBOOKING ARE SPARSE BY DESIGN
Both mechanisms have narrow stage windows — no-show fires at the S5/S6 arrival gate; overbooking fires at S4 detection and S6 walk-in. Outside those windows the conditions cannot physically exist. The sparseness is a feature, not incompleteness: the grid shows where each condition can arise, not every stage through which a resulting entry might later flow.
4.6
AI agent authority model — rendered as a single conceptual view

The AI Communication Agent sits at L0 with Tier 1 authority: it classifies inbound intent, drafts communication, and proposes system actions. It cannot approve its own drafts, cannot execute governed actions, and — under the v2.3 clarification — cannot send outbound communications under any trust setting, including FULL_AUTO. Trust levels are configured by the GM at L4 (Admin Console) and apply only to internal system actions. The three FORBIDDENs are absolute, not graduated.

WHY THREE ABSOLUTES, NOT GRADUATED
The AI agent has a wide operational surface — it can read every inbound message, draft every outbound one, and see into every record. The only defensible posture, given that surface, is categorical refusal of autonomous action. Graduated trust levels would mean the AI could commit the hotel under some configurations and not others; the system would then need a meta-rule governing which configurations are safe. Absolute refusal removes that whole failure mode.
THE v2.3 CLARIFICATION (MOST-MISUNDERSTOOD PART)
Before v2.3 the FULL_AUTO trust level was ambiguous — it read as if setting it high could delegate outbound communications to the AI. The v2.3 change makes the distinction sharp: trust levels automate internal actions only. No combination of settings, no seniority of configurer, no volume of prior correct drafts can lift the outbound-review requirement. Outbound to a guest is outbound to the world — and the world is a place where silence is better than an AI going unilateral.
HOW 4.6 RELATES TO 4.2
Layer 4.2 lists the three AI-agent FORBIDDENs as abstract rules in the "AI agent" cluster of the global forbidden map. 4.6 renders them as a working model — an actor with capabilities fanning one way and blocks fanning the other, plus the trust-level configuration surface that applies only to the inner domain. Same rules, two views: 4.2 for comprehension alongside other system-wide forbiddens; 4.6 for understanding the AI's operational shape.
05
Layer Five · Data & State Machines

The machines beneath the lifecycle —
how state actually moves, and how it is sealed.

Every governed entity in LEGPHEL has an explicit state machine. This layer renders the two machines that everything else hangs off — the entry lifecycle (the engagement's own composite status × stage model) and the folio lifecycle (the financial container's six-state enum) — as first-class diagrams, not as prose references. Arrows are not drawn where they feel natural; they are drawn where the governing state-transition matrix places them. Part A — core state machines (delivered this session): 5.1 entry lifecycle with forward, re-entry, parking, and terminal paths; 5.2 folio lifecycle with the stage-aligned transition map and post-closure access model. Part B — inventory & dispute (delivered this session): 5.3 inventory claim state renders the ten-state Room.currentClaimState machine with its QUOTED displacement threshold, the OCCUPIED↛DEPARTED_CLEAN forbidden pattern, and the RoomClaimStateEvent audit footer; 5.4 dispute state machine renders the five-state DisputeRecord.state machine with the DISPUTE_EXHAUSTED non-state made explicit and the S7→S8 / S8→S9 gate panel as its architecturally distinctive feature.

5.1
Entry lifecycle state machine · composite (status × stage)

The state of an entry is a composite (Entry.status, Entry.currentStage) — two fields that decouple on re-entry. Forward flow advances the engagement; re-entry transitions create new segments (except S8→S7, which returns for charge posting without sealing); parking suspends the lifecycle while preserving the stage; three terminal states — CLOSED, CANCELLED, EXPIRED — close the entry. The S5 no-show path is a sub-process: the entry remains in (ACTIVE, S5) throughout and routes through S9-equivalent closure to CLOSED — the NO_SHOW_CLOSED state lives on the folio side (see 5.2), not on the entry. Authority per transition is covered in 4.3 and 4.5.

Why the composite (status, stage) model exists
Four concepts — stage, status, parked, terminal — must never be collapsed. Stage determines what actions the system permits right now. Status captures operational and commercial characterisations that may persist across stage changes (a reservation is still CONFIRMED even when it is being reconfigured through an S4→S1 re-entry). Parked is a non-terminal suspension with preserved stage. Terminal is a governed end condition. Collapsing any two produces ambiguity downstream systems cannot resolve deterministically.
Re-entry creates new segments, not edits
Every re-entry transition (with the single exception of S8→S7) creates a new Segment record. The prior segment is sealed and read-only. Entry.segmentNumber is incremented. The ReEntryConsequenceEngine computes consequence payloads on segment creation. This is the replicate-and-revalidate doctrine — silent mutation of prior commercial reality is the forbidden pattern.
Terminal ≠ "nothing more to do"
A terminal state is a governed end condition with preserved history, defined residue handling, and known financial settlement. CLOSED means all S9 obligations resolved (commission-due record created conditionally — only when the agent profile has a commission rate configured; B11-001 correction). CANCELLED preserves history; financial residue is governed. EXPIRED is recorded, not silent. A CANCELLED entry does not reactivate — a returning guest starts a new entry. The governing state-transition matrix is authoritative: three terminals, not four.
No-show is a sub-process · folio-side terminal is on 5.2
The entry does not acquire a no-show terminal state. It remains (ACTIVE, S5) throughout the no-show sub-process — NO_SHOW_PENDING → AWAITING_WRITTEN_CONFIRMATION / NO_SHOW_DETERMINED (FOM) — then routes through S9-equivalent closure to CLOSED. The folio's counterpart transition — PROVISIONAL → NO_SHOW_CLOSED — is rendered in 5.2. Both sides are required for a complete picture: the entry closes on the entry side; the financial residue is sealed on the folio side.
Parking cascade · provenance-governed unpark
Entry-level park is independent. Inquiry-level park cascades to all ACTIVE non-terminal children. The subtlety is the unpark rule: inquiry-level unpark does NOT resume entries that were individually parked before the cascade. Each entry retains its per-entry park provenance, and the unpark evaluator respects it. This is the LEGPHEL-specific guarantee — the system must maintain per-entry park provenance to evaluate this rule correctly.
5.2
Folio lifecycle state machine · aligned to the stage axis

The folio is the financial container that mirrors the entry's commercial commitments but follows its own state machine. Six states — two active (PROVISIONAL, LIVE) and four terminal (SETTLED, NO_SHOW_CLOSED, OUTSTANDING, CANCELLED) — governed by six transitions and one forbidden pattern: a LIVE folio may never revert to PROVISIONAL. The stage alignment axis below the state machine bridges this diagram to 5.1 — each transition fires at a specific lifecycle stage. Post-closure access is additive only: credits, corrections, and GM adjustments add new lines; original lines are never modified.

Why LIVE never reverts to PROVISIONAL
The LIVE state represents the folio's transition from commercial promise to operational execution — billing model activated, charges being posted, a real financial record in motion. Reverting to PROVISIONAL would erase that operational reality. Any post-S6 change — rate revision, billing model change, room change, date extension — is handled through a new segment on the entry side (5.1) and additive folio lines on the folio side. This is the folio equivalent of silent-mutation-forbidden: the financial history is append-only. Amendment never means reversion.
The four terminal states are not interchangeable
SETTLED means balance cleared — no residue, no follow-up. OUTSTANDING means the closure event fired with a governed-outstanding balance; the folio is sealed but the payment follow-up timer is active and will escalate to FOM then GM. NO_SHOW_CLOSED is a pre-arrival terminal — penalty posted, advance reconciled, refund tracked if surplus. CANCELLED is a pre-S6 cancellation — financial residue governed by cancellation policy. Each has distinct downstream consequences for reporting, reconciliation, and audit. Collapsing them is the forbidden pattern from Part 1.
Append-only corrections · audit integrity
Post-closure access is additive, not overwrite. Every correction leaves a new line with its own timestamp, actor, and rationale. FOM adds credit notes and adjustments at Level 2; GM corrections at Level 3 carry their own date and rationale. The original lines are permanently visible in the ledger. This is the same append-only pattern as night audit records and TraceEvents — irreversibility is the mechanism by which audit integrity is maintained. A folio that could be edited in place could not be audited; a folio that can only be amended through new lines is self-auditing by construction.
5.3
Inventory claim state machine · Room.currentClaimState

Inventory is not a boolean of "available or not." Every room carries a claim state — a date-range-evaluated enum that records, at any given moment, how the room's capacity is promised. Ten states, fifteen transitions, one forbidden pattern. The commercial lifecycle runs along the upper row (FREE → QUOTED → SPECULATIVELY_HELD → COMMITTED_HELD → CONFIRMED); the stay cycle runs beneath (OCCUPIED → DEPARTED_DIRTY → DEPARTED_CLEAN, then back to FREE). Two exception states sit off-axis. One transition is explicitly forbidden — OCCUPIED ↛ DEPARTED_CLEAN bypasses housekeeping governance. Every state change produces an immutable RoomClaimStateEvent.

Why claim state is separate from physical state
Inventory Claim and Room Physical are two different machines describing the same room from two different angles. Claim state is a date-range-evaluated enum on Room.currentClaimState — it records how the room's capacity is promised (FREE, QUOTED, held, confirmed, occupied, post-stay, blocked, under maintenance). Physical state is a boolean-flag derivation from Room.isBlocked, Room.isUnderMaintenance, and currentClaimState — it records what the room physically is at this moment (available, occupied, dirty, clean, blocked, maintenance). They interact but are not the same machine. 5.3 renders the claim state machine; physical state does not need its own state-machine diagram because it is a derivation, not an enum with guards.
Why OCCUPIED → DEPARTED_CLEAN is forbidden
The housekeeping-governance mandate at is not cosmetic. DEPARTED_DIRTY is the state in which the housekeeping actor records the completion event — it is the mechanism by which housekeeping's audit record is produced. A direct OCCUPIED → DEPARTED_CLEAN transition would bypass the event log entirely; there would be no record of who cleaned the room, when, or what they observed. Housekeeping governance requires the intermediate. This forbiddenness is the same shape as 5.2's LIVE ↛ PROVISIONAL and 3c's OTA→DELIBERATE reclass forbiddenness — a governance mechanism hard-wired into the state machine rather than left to service-layer enforcement.
QUOTED displacement threshold doctrine
The QUOTED → SPECULATIVELY_HELD transition is the only transition on this machine that carries an approval gate in its middle (REV-B3-01). The threshold is configurable per property. Below the threshold, the displacement proceeds silently — it is a quotation being overridden by a hold, which is a routine commercial event. Above the threshold, the FOM/GM approval gate fires before the transition takes effect. The important doctrinal point: the threshold is not a hard block. It is an approval trigger. The transition is always possible; what varies is whether authority is required to make it happen.
Claim state history is never lost
Room.currentClaimState is a derived field — it reflects the current resolved state for the current date. The actual truth lives in the RoomClaimStateEvent log. Every transition — forward, return, exception, cycle-close — produces one immutable event carrying the seven fields in the audit strip above. This means full claim state history for any date range can be reconstructed from the event log; the "current state" field is a convenience view. This is the same append-only pattern as the folio ledger and night audit records — irreversibility is the mechanism by which audit integrity is maintained. A claim state that could be overwritten in place could not be audited; a claim state machine whose mutations are the sole authorised mechanism of change is self-auditing by construction.
5.4
Dispute state machine · DisputeRecord.state

A dispute is not a state of an entry — it is its own first-class record, with its own five-state lifecycle and its own stage-gate interaction. Five states, seven transitions, one non-state made explicit. The architecturally distinctive feature is not on the state diagram itself but on the gate panel below it: the S7 → S8 gate returns with a GM override path available; the S8 → S9 gate has no override path. An entry with an unresolved dispute at S8 may not close. This is the single hardest rule the dispute machine enforces.

Why DISPUTE_EXHAUSTED is not a valid state
The intuition that "the guest declined every offered resolution" deserves its own terminal state is wrong. and explicitly exclude DISPUTE_EXHAUSTED from the enum. The outcome is modelled as CLOSED with a mandatory recorded reason ("guest declined all offered resolutions"). The distinction matters: a separate DISPUTE_EXHAUSTED state would produce a terminal class the system could not distinguish from formal GM closure — reporting, reconciliation, and audit would all have to treat the two the same way, because they carry identical information. Collapsing them into CLOSED-with-reason means the system records why the dispute closed without inventing a second terminal that duplicates the first. The ghosted bubble on this diagram exists to make the absence explicit — developers who assume DISPUTE_EXHAUSTED should exist are the audience for the STOP glyph.
Why S8 → S9 has no override path
The S7 → S8 gate allows a GM override because an entry in S7 is still live — the guest has not yet checked out, and the operational mechanics of their stay must continue even while a dispute is under management. Blocking S7 → S8 would mean a guest's entry could not progress from pre-arrival to check-in because of an open commercial grievance, which would hurt the guest, not protect them. The S8 → S9 boundary is different: S9 is the entry's natural terminal state. A dispute in OPEN or IN_PROGRESS at the moment of entry closure would leave a permanent unresolved commercial grievance attached to a sealed entry, producing an audit-breaking class of record — a closed entry with an active dispute, forever. The system forbids this by construction: S8 → S9 is the one stage boundary on which the dispute machine holds veto power, no GM override, no service-layer workaround. If the dispute cannot be resolved or formally closed, the entry cannot close.
REOPENED is not a new dispute
The CLOSED → REOPENED → IN_PROGRESS cycle preserves the dispute identity. A dispute that is reopened is the same DisputeRecord, now carrying a new transition in its event log, not a new record replacing the old. This matters for audit integrity: reopen events, their reasons, their authorising actors, and the work done during the re-engagement are all attributable to the original dispute. If reopen produced a new DisputeRecord with its own identity, the link between the original grievance and its second engagement would be a service-layer convention rather than a data-model invariant — which is the forbidden pattern from Part 1. Append-only, identity-preserving, reason-recorded: these three properties together are how the dispute machine survives the reopen cycle with its audit trail intact.

Layer Six · Cross-Stage Mechanics

The machines that don't live inside a stage —
how charges travel, how re-entry is shaped, when audit fires, what every timer governs.

Layers 1–5 treat each stage as a locus. Layer 6 treats the mechanisms that cut across the S1–S9 axis. 6.1 traces the financial flow from the provisional folio at S2 through every charge category that originates at one stage and lands at another. 6.2 renders the three architecturally-distinct shapes of re-entry — amendment-driven, parking, and the singular S8→S7 checkout return — distinguished by the segment and currentStage invariants. 6.3 opens the night audit cross-stage mechanism: run timeline, per-entry transaction loop, failure recovery, and per-stage impact. 6.4 plots all thirty-seven governed timers on a single stage axis with their owning workers and escalation chains surfaced. Together these four close the cross-stage view of the system.

6.1
Folio flow cross-stage · posting lanes on the stage axis

The folio's lifecycle (rendered in 5.2) tells you what states the folio passes through. This diagram tells you when charges actually land on it. Five posting lanes fire at different stages, write different line types, and follow different authority chains — yet every line ultimately lands on a single folio record per entry. The rate-snapshot invariance rule (rate frozen at S4, never re-resolved at posting time) governs the entire ROOM_CHARGE lane. The NO_SHOW branch at S5 is architecturally distinctive: the provisional folio closes as NO_SHOW_CLOSED without ever converting to live.

WHY POSTING ≠ FOLIO STATE
5.2 shows the folio's six states — PROVISIONAL, LIVE, SETTLED, OUTSTANDING, NO_SHOW_CLOSED, sealed. This diagram shows something different: the five posting lanes that write lines onto whichever folio exists. The folio's state machine governs what the folio is; the posting lanes govern what gets added to it, when, and under what authority. Both views are necessary — neither substitutes for the other.
RATE-SNAPSHOT INVARIANCE
The ROOM_CHARGE lane is governed by a single hard rule: the rate posted nightly is Reservation.frozenRate, captured in the S4 commitment snapshot. Night audit never re-resolves the rate. If the rate needs to change, it goes through the amendment path (Path 3 full renegotiation — see 6.2), which creates a new segment with its own frozen rate. This rule is why forbids "applying a rate change without re-running the PricingPipelineEngine."
NO_SHOW RECONCILIATION
At S5, if the guest does not arrive, the folio takes a permanent branch: it closes as NO_SHOW_CLOSED without ever converting to LIVE. The penalty is posted as a line referencing the NoShowDeterminationRecord.id as operational anchor (FOM determination timestamp, not expected-arrival date). The advance payment then reconciles against the penalty — if equal, settled; if surplus, a refund record is created; if shortfall, it posts as outstanding. This closure type is distinct from SETTLED, OUTSTANDING, and CANCELLED.
POST-CLOSURE CORRECTIONS ARE APPEND-ONLY
Once the folio seals at S9, no original FolioLine is ever edited. Corrections are additive entries: Level 2 (FOM) adds lines, Level 3 (GM) adds corrections carrying their own transactionDate. A sealed NightAuditRecord is similarly immutable — a charge mistakenly missed from a past operating date is posted today with a referenceDate pointer to the original date. Temporal integrity is preserved by the two-date model, never by backdating.
6.2
Re-entry topology · the three architecturally-distinct shapes

"Re-entry" is not one mechanism — it is three shapes that differ on two invariants: does a new segment get created? and does Entry.currentStage revert? Amendment-driven re-entry answers yes · yes across twelve rows of the composite state. Parking answers no · no — the entry suspends in place. S8 → S7 checkout return is the singular no · yes exception: the one re-entry row in the entire consequence matrix that returns a prior stage without sealing the current segment. All three preserve identity and audit history.

AMENDMENT RE-ENTRY IS THE GENERAL CASE
Twelve of thirteen rows in the re-entry matrix create a new segment. This is the pattern: date change, rate change, room change, billing-model change, and commercial renegotiation all produce a new segment that seals the prior one read-only and computes a consequence payload (timers, folio, holds, handoffs, inventory). AmendmentService orchestrates via three paths — simple folio amendment (Path 1, no segment), commercial amendment (Path 2, no segment), and full renegotiation (Path 3, new segment). The consequence matrix is authoritative; the paths are the routing layer.
PARKING IS NOT A RE-ENTRY IN SEGMENT TERMS
Parking is the only shape on this diagram where Entry.currentStage does not change at any point. The entry suspends at its current stage and resumes there on unpark. The architectural subtlety is unpark provenance — inquiry-level unpark does not unpark entries that were individually parked before the inquiry-level park. The system must maintain per-entry park provenance to evaluate this correctly. The only way a parked entry terminates without unpark is ENTRY_EXPIRY past the governing timer threshold.
S8 → S7 IS THE SINGULAR EXCEPTION
In the full re-entry consequence matrix, every row's "New Segment" column reads Yes — except row 12. The S8 → S7 return for additional charge posting is the one re-entry shape that does not create a new segment. This is not a design oversight; it is deliberate. A minibar charge discovered at checkout does not warrant the ceremony of a new segment — the guest's stay has not materially changed. The charge posts to the live folio, then the entry re-advances to S8. The architectural distinction is what makes it a column on this diagram rather than a row in Column 1.
6.3
Night audit cross-stage mechanics · the nightly pass

Night audit is the single most architecturally dense mechanism in the system. It fires every operating night, touches every active entry, posts the daily ROOM_CHARGE on the rate frozen at S4, runs the missing-expected-charges completeness check, and seals an immutable NightAuditRecord. Its concurrency model is idempotency — not a lock. A failed run writes PARTIAL status with the unprocessed entries enumerated; the recovery re-run is safe because the engine skips entries with a posted FolioLine already carrying the operating date. Entries that cannot be processed even through recovery raise AUDIT_EXCEPTION and escalate to FOM.

IDEMPOTENCY IS NOT LOCKING
A lock prevents concurrent access; idempotency tolerates it. Night audit uses idempotency guards at two levels — top-level (skip whole run if a COMPLETE record exists) and per-entry (skip individual entry if the ROOM_CHARGE FolioLine for operatingDate already exists). A failed run can therefore be re-run at any time without double-posting, without a lock being held, and without a reconciliation step. This is why night audit does not interact with ProcessingLock — they solve different problems.
SYSTEM-DATE IS NOT WALL-CLOCK MIDNIGHT
operatingDate is a governed business day, not UTC midnight. Night audit is scheduled to fire after the day's operational activity is complete — typically 2 AM local time, but configurable per property. The critical invariant is that night audit must complete before the next operating day's financial transactions begin. Rollover is a governed event: the sealed NightAuditRecord is the financial truth for that date, and the next day's transactions carry forward from that sealed state.
WHY NIGHT AUDIT IS NOT A BATCH JOB
A batch job processes a bounded set of inputs and produces outputs once. Night audit is different in three architectural respects. First, its output is a financial authority record — the sealed NightAuditRecord is authoritative for the operating day, not a report. Second, it has governed recovery semantics — PARTIAL run status, idempotent re-run, AUDIT_EXCEPTION, FOM and GM escalation chains. Third, it is the single mechanism that touches every active entry in every active stage simultaneously — no other worker or service has that scope. It is the heartbeat of the financial operations, not a scheduled chore.
6.4
Timer Engine surfaces · every governed timer in one view

The Timer Engine is the governance backbone for all time-driven events. Every timer in the system — there are thirty-seven — is registered in a single hardcoded registry and dispatched through pg-boss to a specific worker. No service schedules its own timers outside this mechanism. This diagram plots each timer against the S1–S9 axis at its stage of origin, colour-coded by the category it serves, with its owning worker named at the right. The escalation-chain panel at the bottom shows which timers cascade; the forbidden-patterns panel shows what is architecturally excluded.

WHY EVERY TIMER LIVES IN ONE ENGINE
Centralisation is the governance guarantee. If services owned their own timers, silent expiry would be impossible to prevent — there would be no uniform fire sequence, no uniform audit trail, and no uniform recovery on restart. By registering every timer in one registry and routing every fire through pg-boss to a named worker, the system guarantees that (a) every governed deadline has a single implementation path, (b) every fire produces a TimerEvent record, and (c) timer state survives application restart via pg-boss's persistent job queue. This is infrastructure as governance.
ESCALATION IS APPEND-ONLY, NEVER SILENT
Every escalation step — tier 1 notify, tier 2 notify, FOM escalation, GM escalation, write-off consideration — produces an audit event. The escalation chains in the panel above are not UI flows; they are governed audit sequences. A timer that fires and escalates without producing a record is a defect, not a feature. The operational doctrine is: you can always reconstruct from the audit log which notifications fired, when, and to whom.
TIMER STATE SURVIVES RESTART
pg-boss persists all scheduled jobs in a PostgreSQL table. If the application restarts mid-flight, pg-boss recovers the job queue on startup — no timer is lost. The TimerRecord state and the pg-boss job state are kept consistent by the registration and fire handlers. Durability is not a best-effort property here; it is the architectural foundation. This is why node-native setTimeout and setInterval are forbidden — they evaporate on restart and carry no audit record.
